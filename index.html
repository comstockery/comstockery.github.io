visualizing the use of samples in J Dilla's hip-hop classic

J DILLA
DONUTS
(2006)

"[Donuts is] just a compilation of the stuff I thought was a little too much for the MCs. That’s basically what it is...me flipping records that people really don’t know how to rap on but they want to rap on." —J Dilla,
Scratch Magazine interview,
published February 2006

J Dilla's Donuts is an album composed entirely of samples. What started as a beat tape shared among friends was finalized in the final year of J Dilla's life. The album was released by Stones Throw Records on J Dilla's 32nd birthday: February 7, 2006. J Dilla died three days later.

Sampling Donuts combines information gathered from WhoSampled—a user-populated database of known hip-hop samples—as well as applied data attributes for the timing, duration, and type of each sample's use.

"[J Dilla] played them a new CD of beats... He'd given it one of his playful titles that derived from the name of some unhealthy food that he shouldn't be eating in his condition, like Burger King, Pizza Man, or in this case, Donuts." —Dan Charnas,
Dilla Time,
published February 2022

Of the 10 genre categories available on WhoSampled, more than half of the samples used on Donuts are soul / funk / disco samples from the late 1960s or early 1970s. Some of the earliest samples are from Raymond Scott, a pioneer of early electronic music. Almost all of the samples since the early 1980s are hip-hop samples, both from the Golden Age of Hip-Hop (which was effectively ended with the lawsuit against Biz Markie) and from then-contemporary hip-hop artists of the early 2000s.

"But the advent of sampling yielded a significant change: because more than one loop could now be played simultaneously, producers could take their drums and their music from different records. With samplers, any music could be combined with a great drum pattern to make what is essentially a composite break. Moreover, different loops (and "stabs"—short bursts of sound) could be brought in and taken out at different times. This substantially broadened the spectrum of music that could be pressed into service for hip-hop. —Joseph G. Schloss,
Making Beats: The Art of Sample-Based Hip-Hop,
published 2004

WhoSampled provides information on which elements were used in a sample, but labeling a sample as a sound effect, hook, or drum may not capture how it's used in a composition. Musicologist Dr. Amanda Sewell proposed a typology for hip-hop samples to categorize them as:

  • STRUCTURAL samples, which form the harmonic and rhythmic basis of a track
  • SURFACE samples, which decorate and punctuate the structural samples, and
  • LYRIC samples, which contain comprehensible words or phrases.

Almost every track on Donuts uses one structural sample, as J Dilla rarely used material from multiple sources to compose the harmonic and rythmic basis. Despite being considered an instrumental album, almost every track includes at least one lyric sample, many of which are referenced in the track names.

WORKINONIT
STRUCTURAL 10cc - Worst Band in the World
SURFACE Mantronix - King of the Beats 10cc - Worst Band in the World "Sweet" Charles Sherrell - Yes It's You Raymond Scott - Sprite: Mellonball Bounce Malcolm McLaren - Buffalo Gals
LYRIC Beastie Boys - The New Style 10cc - Worst Band in the World unknown Joeski Love - Pee-Wee's Dance Skillz and Ras Kass - Six Figures

Workinonit, the longest and most complex track on Donuts, presents the album in minature form. Almost every sample reappears later in the album.

The Mantronix siren that kicks off the track is the most common sample on the album. That siren, from King of the Beats, hypes up various points of eight other tracks on Donuts.

Lyric samples from the Beastie Boys' The New Style reappear in The New and the moans from "Sweet" Charles Sherell's Yes It's You show up in Time: The Donut of the Heart. The holler from Malcolm McLaren's Buffalo Gals and Joeski Love's "huh? what?" are also used in The Twister (Huh, What).

STOP
STRUCTURAL Dionne Warwick - You're Gonna Need Me
SURFACE Skillz and Ras Kass - Six Figures
LYRIC Jadakiss feat. Anthony Hamilton - Why Dionne Warwick - You're Gonna Need Me Skillz and Ras Kass - Six Figures

One of the most bittersweet tracks on the album, Stop is built around Dianne Warwick's You're Gonna Need Me, with the lyrics "you're gonna want me back / in your arms." The track starts with Jadakiss's laugh and a verbal illusion: Dilla transforms Jadakiss's "it's dat real!" to what you might hear as "is death real?"

This track contains the only fraction of silence on the album: about one minute into Stop, after Dianne sings "you better stop," the music...stops.

PEOPLE
STRUCTURAL Eddie Kendricks - My People...Hold On
SURFACE Eddie Kendricks - My People...Hold On Asha Bhosle - Mujhe Maar Daalo Mantronix - King of the Beats
LYRIC Eddie Kendricks - My People...Hold On Run DMC - Here We Go (Live at the Funhouse)

Dilla's use of different parts of former Temptations member Eddie Kendricks's My People...Hold On are perfect examples for the sample typology: he uses the drums in the introduction of the song as structural component, the phased heavy breathing as surface samples that come in and out of the track, and the vocals of "my people! hold on!" as a relatively straightforward lyric sample.

The song Mujhe Maar Daalo, sung by Asha Bhosle, is from the 1974 Indian film Geetaa Mera Naam, or Geetaa is my Name. Around the same time Donuts was baking, Dilla's friend and collaborator Madlib was producing his Beat Konducta in India album, which also heavily samples from Bollywood music.

LIGHTWORKS
STRUCTURAL Raymond Scott - Lightworks
SURFACE Raymond Scott - Bendix the Tomorrow People Mantronix - King of the Beats Skillz and Ras Kass - Six Figures Raymond Scott - Lightworks
LYRIC Raymond Scott - Lightworks Skillz and Ras Kass - Six Figures

Lightworks might be the only track on the album that only uses samples from electronic and hip-hop sources. Dilla uses two songs from Raymond Scott, a pioneer of electronic music and inventor of several electronic instruments.

Dilla creates another verbal illusion when he transforms the lyrics "light up the skies" in Raymond Scott's Lightworks into "light up the spliffs." The voice we hear is that of Dorothy Collins, a singer and then-wife of Raymond Scott. Dorothy often worked as a spokeswoman and vocalist in TV commercials for Lucky Strike cigarettes in the late 1950s.
